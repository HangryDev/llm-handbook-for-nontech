# Framing

This is a beta test, not a launch event.

The 10 attendees are already on the email list.
List building is not the goal.

The goal is to extract honest signal on three things:

1. **Does the teaching format work?** — pace, structure, depth, hands-on vs. lecture balance
2. **Does the content land?** — is this the right topic, framed the right way, for the right people?
3. **What do they want next?** — what would they pay for, in what format, at what price?

The pilot also produces a fourth output that is separate from the beta test:

4. **A reference package for lecturer agencies.**
Starting in April, outreach begins to external corporate training platforms.
They will ask for proof that you can teach — a recording, attendee responses, and a course description.
The March 28 session is the only opportunity to produce that proof before outreach begins.
If the recording or testimonials are missing, the April outreach has nothing to show.

경재's primary job during the session is not logistics.
It is **observation** — watching the room while 한결 teaches.

---

# 경재/지현's Role: Live Observation

While 한결 runs the session, 지현/경재 tracks the room.

Use a simple sheet divided into two columns: **engaged** / **lost**.

At each major transition in the session (topic change, exercise start, Q&A), note:
- Who is leaning in, asking questions, taking notes
- Who has gone quiet, seems confused, or has disengaged
- Any moment where the energy in the room dropped

This is not about judging individuals.
It is about identifying **which part of the content** creates engagement and which part loses people.

Share observations with 한결 immediately after the session, before debriefing attendees.

---

# Interview Structure

The feedback is collected in two stages.

**Stage 1 — Immediate reaction (end of session, 10 minutes)**
Group format. Open questions to the whole room.
Quick and emotional — first impressions before people overthink.

**Stage 2 — Individual debrief (within 3 days after)**
One-on-one. Can be a short call, voice message, or written form.
This is where the real answers come out.

Both stages use the questions below.

---

# Stage 1 — Immediate Group Questions
### End of session, open to the room

Keep this to 3 questions. Do not over-structure the ending.

**Q1. What was the one thing that surprised you most?**

Purpose: Surfaces what was unexpected — either positively or negatively.
What to listen for: If multiple people say the same thing, that is your strongest content signal.

**Q2. What felt unclear or hard to follow?**

Purpose: Identify the weakest part of the teaching format.
Normalize this — say "I'm testing the format today, not your understanding."
What to listen for: Anything that more than one person mentions is a real problem to fix.

**Q3. If you had to describe what this session was about in one sentence to a colleague, what would you say?**

Purpose: Tests whether your positioning is landing.
What to listen for: Does their one-sentence match what you intended? If not, your framing needs work.

---

# Stage 2 — Individual Debrief Questions
### Follow-up within 3 days

These are the questions that matter most.
Do them one-on-one — people give different answers in private than in a group.
A 15-minute call is ideal. A written form works if calls are difficult to schedule.

---

## Section A: Content Validation

**Q4. What part of today's session are you most likely to actually use in your work this week?**

Purpose: Separates "interesting" from "useful." You want useful.
What to listen for: If people can answer this quickly and specifically, the content is practical enough. If they hesitate or give vague answers, the content may be too conceptual.

**Q5. Was there a moment where you thought — "I wish we had gone deeper on this"?**

Purpose: Identifies where demand exceeds what you delivered.
What to listen for: These are your future content topics and product ideas.

**Q6. Was there anything covered that felt irrelevant to your work?**

Purpose: Identifies what to cut.
What to listen for: Anything multiple people flag as irrelevant should be removed or repositioned.

---

## Section B: Audience and Problem Research

**Q7. What is the biggest AI-related problem you are trying to solve in your work right now?**

Purpose: Market research. Not about today's session — about the real problem space.
What to listen for: Patterns across multiple people. If 6 of 10 say a version of the same problem, that is your next product.

**Q8. What have you already tried, and why didn't it fully work?**

Purpose: Understand the gap between what exists and what people actually need.
What to listen for: Frustrations with existing tools, courses, or resources are your competitive positioning.

---

## Section C: Demand and Pricing Signal

**Q9. If today's session were a paid workshop, what would feel like a fair price?**

Do not suggest a number first. Let them answer.

Purpose: Direct pricing research for Cohort 2.
What to listen for: The range they give. If most people say 50,000–100,000 KRW unprompted, your Cohort 2 price should be in that range. If they say 200,000+, you have more room than you thought.

**Q10. If there were a more in-depth version of this — say, 4 sessions over a month with exercises and feedback — would you join? What would make you say yes or no?**

Purpose: Tests appetite for the cohort format specifically.
What to listen for: "Yes, if..." answers are the most valuable. The conditions they attach tell you exactly what Cohort 2 needs to include to convert them.

**Q11. Would you prefer to learn this in a live group format, a self-paced course, or an ongoing community?**

Purpose: Format research for the offer ladder.
What to listen for: If the majority say self-paced, that shifts when you build the online course. If they say live group, the cohort model is validated. If they say community, that accelerates the community decision from September.

---

## Section D: Referral and Social Proof

**Q12. Is there a specific person — a colleague, peer, or friend — who you think should have been in the room today?**

Purpose: Two things at once — referral signal and ideal customer profile.
What to listen for: Who they name and what role that person has. This tells you where your real audience is and whether word-of-mouth is likely to work.

**Q13. Would you be willing to share one sentence about what you got from today, for use as a testimonial?**

Purpose: Social proof for Cohort 2 promotion.
This should be the last question — ask after the conversation has built trust.

---

# After the Interviews: What to Look For

Compile the answers across all 10 people and look for patterns.

| Question cluster | What a strong signal looks like | What a weak signal looks like |
|---|---|---|
| Content validation (Q4–Q6) | Most people name the same useful thing | Everyone names something different; hard to see a pattern |
| Problem research (Q7–Q8) | 5+ people describe the same core problem | 10 completely different problems |
| Pricing (Q9) | Unprompted estimates cluster in a range | Huge spread (some say 10,000, some say 500,000) |
| Format (Q11) | Clear majority preference | Evenly split — means you may need to test two formats |
| Referral (Q12) | They name someone immediately and specifically | They say "I'm not sure" — weak referral likelihood |

---

# What to Do With the Results

Bring the compiled answers into the June cohort review.

They answer the four questions that determine Cohort 2:

1. What is the content? (Q4, Q5, Q6, Q7)
2. Who is it for? (Q7, Q8, Q12)
3. What format? (Q11)
4. What price? (Q9, Q10)

If the answers are unclear or contradictory across the 10 people, that is signal too.
It means the audience is not yet well-defined, and Cohort 2 should be designed to narrow the target before scaling.

## Lecturer Agency Reference Package
*To be assembled within one week of the pilot.*

This package is what you send when an external training platform asks "can you show me what you do?"

- [ ] **Session recording edited** — full session, clean audio, screen capture of demo included. Does not need to be polished. It needs to be watchable.
- [ ] **60-second highlight clip** — the clearest, most compelling 60 seconds of the session. Used as a quick proof of teaching quality.
- [ ] **One-page course description** — title, target audience, duration, what participants will be able to do afterward. One page, no more.
- [ ] **3 written testimonials collected** — from the Stage 2 individual debriefs (Q13). One sentence each. Name and job title if they consent.
- [ ] **Speaker profile** — 한결's bio in two lengths: 3 sentences and one paragraph. Focused on applied AI expertise, not credentials.

## The two things most likely to cause problems

**1. Audio**
Bad audio makes the recording unusable as a content asset.
Fix this before the day — not on it.

**2. Cloud instance access under load**
Test the venue wifi with multiple devices hitting the instances simultaneously.
If it can't handle it, either get a backup connection or reduce the hands-on portion to a demo-only format.
