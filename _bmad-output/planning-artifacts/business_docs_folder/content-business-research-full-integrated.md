# Executive Summary

This research focused on people and businesses that teach a **content → course → paid community** business model, especially relevant for a future path similar to:

- creator-led teaching
- selling courses / cohorts
- building a community business like Skool
- eventually layering consulting, mastermind, or other premium offers on top

Across the strongest examples, the recurring business model looks like:

```text
content
→ audience
→ email list
→ low-ticket digital product
→ cohort course
→ paid community
→ consulting / mastermind
```

The most relevant models for this path were:

- Justin Welsh — content systems + digital products + solopreneur monetization
- Pat Flynn — course creation roadmap + community-powered education
- Stu McLaren — membership-first business model, pricing, launch, retention
- Jay Clouse — membership design, tiering, creator community economics
- Ali Abdaal — creator education business with strong content engine and systems
- Sean Cannell — YouTube education → course → coaching membership ladder
- Roberto Blake — diversified creator monetization stack
- Codie Sanchez — media-first → education/community → larger business ecosystem
- Gina Bianchini — community design / community economics / retention logic

A core distinction emerged:

- Some people treat **community as the product**
- Others treat **community as a retention / implementation layer around courses**

This distinction matters for business design.

The deep research prioritized:

- official websites

The evaluation lens used three practical dimensions:

# Partnership and Revenue Split Discussion (from the chat)

This section captures the partnership / working-together discussion that came up alongside the research.

## Base scenario discussed
You create and teach the course.  
A partner brings people through marketing / audience and may also handle operations.

This is a **Creator + Growth/Operator** structure.

## Important framing
Do **not** split by person first.  
Split by **function** first.

Recommended functional buckets:

| Function | Description |
|---|---|
| Content | curriculum / materials / course creation |
| Teaching | delivery / facilitation / expertise |
| Distribution | marketing / audience / promotion / customer acquisition |
| Operations | onboarding / admin / community management / logistics |

A sample functional allocation discussed:

| Function | Example weight |
|---|---:|
| Content | 30% |
| Teaching | 20% |
| Distribution | 30% |
| Operations | 20% |

Then assign people into those buckets.

## Common split patterns discussed

### 1. Creator-centric model
When the course, expertise, and brand are mostly yours:

| Role | Typical share |
|---|---:|
| Creator | 50–60% |
| Growth/Operator partner | 40–50% |

### 2. Audience-heavy partner model
If the partner already owns distribution and brings the real audience:

| Role | Typical share |
|---|---:|
| Creator | 40–50% |
| Audience / Growth partner | 50–60% |

### 3. Affiliate-like model
If the partner is mostly a seller / promoter and not a true operator:

| Role | Typical share |
|---|---:|
| Creator | 60–80% |
| Affiliate / promoter | 20–40% |

## Example practical splits discussed

### Option A — good default when you teach and own the content
| Person | Share |
|---|---:|
| You | 60% |
| Partner | 40% |

Use when:
- you create the IP
- you teach
- partner handles growth and ops

### Option B — balanced risk-sharing
| Person | Share |
|---|---:|
| You | 50% |
| Partner | 50% |

Use when:
- both share costs
- partner is deeply involved in both growth and operations
- execution burden is truly shared

### Option C — partner has meaningful audience asset
| Person | Share |
|---|---:|
| You | 40% |
| Partner | 60% |

Use when:
- partner’s audience is the main source of demand
- without them, customer acquisition would be dramatically harder

## More complex real-world case discussed

You described a more complex scenario:

- Partner 1: simple operations / but has audience
- Partner 2: unclear, but may handle several functions
- Additional collaborator: may co-create content with you

In that case, the recommendation was to **split by function, then map people to functions**, instead of trying to split person-to-person emotionally.

Example structure discussed:

| Person | Example allocation |
|---|---:|
| You | 40–45 |
| Partner 1 | 25–30 |
| Partner 2 | 15–20 |
| Content collaborator | 10 |

This was not “the” answer, but a reasonable starting template.

## Strong practical recommendation
Do not treat this as permanent equity too early.

Instead, run it by **cohort / launch cycle**.

Example:
- Cohort 1 split
- Cohort 2 split
- Cohort 3 split

Reasons:
- roles may change
- effort may differ
- some people may underperform
- some people may add unexpected value

## Additional revenue split principle discussed
**Distribution is often more valuable than people expect.**  
In many creator businesses, the actual scarce asset is not content creation — it is distribution.

So you should not underprice:
- owned audience
- customer acquisition
- real marketing execution

## Costs and contract points that must be defined
Before splitting revenue, define:

1. Who pays for ads?
2. Are platform fees removed before split?
3. How are refunds handled?
4. Who owns the course IP?
5. Who owns the email list?
6. Who owns the community space?
7. Can the course be resold later?
8. Are recordings reusable?
9. Is the partner paid on gross or net revenue?

## Practical calculation model recommended
Use **net revenue split**.

Example:
- gross revenue = 10,000,000 KRW
- platform fees = 1,000,000 KRW
- ads = 2,000,000 KRW
- net distributable = 7,000,000 KRW

Then split the net amount.

## Strong overall recommendation from the chat
For your situation, a very realistic starting point is:

- You: 60
- Partner: 40

or, in a more multi-person structure:

- You: 45
- Partner 1: 30
- Partner 2: 15
- Content collaborator: 10

Then revisit after the first cohort.
